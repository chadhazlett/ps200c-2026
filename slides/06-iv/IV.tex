\begin{frame}{The Intuition for (Modern) IV}
\onslide<1-> The key intuition for most causal IV work can be thought about in terms of a \textit{randomized encouragement} design. \\
\bigskip
\onslide<2-> Suppose you want to test an important hypothesis: \onslide<3-> \textcolor{Red}{in humans, does pizza consumption improve happiness?}
\small
\begin{itemize} 
\item<4-> Can we just look at a sample and use $\E[happy| pizza]-\E[happy|no\;pizza]$?
\item<5-> Turns out your IRB won't let you randomize, because denying pizza for the controls would be a human rights violation
\item<6-> Instead, suppose we randomly assign half the people in our sample to receive gift cards for pizza. 
\item<7-> Assume we get to observe which ones actually get a pizza in the following week, then measure happiness for everybody in our sample. 
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}


\begin{frame}{The Intuition for (Modern) IV}
\small
\onslide<1-> \textcolor{Blue}{Good news}: we have an experiment w.r.t. our gift card \\ (aka ``encouragement'', ``instrument'') \\

\begin{itemize}
\item<2-> Is the following identifiable? $$ATE_{card}=\E[happy_{1i} - happy_{0i}]$$
%\item<3-> Yes! Just a plain old experiment. 
\item<4-> $ATE_{card}$ called the \textit{reduced form} or \textit{intent to treat} (ITT) effect
\end{itemize}
\medskip
\onslide<5-> But how does this compare to actual causal effect of \textit{pizza} on \textit{happiness}? \\
\medskip
\onslide<6-> \textcolor{Blue}{Answer:} We can back-out effect of the pizza, but only for a certain group.\\
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Backing out effect of pizza:}
 \onslide<1-> \textcolor{Blue}{Intuition}: 
 \footnotesize
 \begin{itemize}
 \item<2-> suppose \textit{card} impacts \textit{happiness} only through \textit{pizza} consumption
 \smallskip
 \item<3-> our entire ITT estimate comes from just the sub-pop for whom \textit{card} changed their \textit{pizza} consumption.
\smallskip 
 \item<4-> $ATE_{card}$ is thus an average of: (a) the effect of $card$ on $happy$ among that group, and (b) a zero effect from those not driven to change their pizza consumption 
\smallskip  
  \item<5-> so $ATE_{card}$ is a possibly ``watered down'' estimate of the effect of pizza. 
 \smallskip
\item<6-> to undo this watering down, we'll see next that we want to divide the $ATE_{card}$ by $Pr(\text{somebody only get pizza because of the card})$, which we can estimate too.
 \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The Intuition for (Modern) IV}
\onslide<1-> Formalizing this, let's consider four groups:
\begin{enumerate}
\footnotesize
\item<2-> \textit{Never-takers.} People with gluten allergies. Won't take the pizza whether they get card or not. \textcolor{Blue}{What is $\E[Y|card]-\E[Y|no\;card]$ for this group?}
\smallskip
\item<3-> \textit{Compliers.} Those who will get the pizza if we give them the card, but not if we don't. Since they follow the encouragement, for them the encouragement \textit{is} the treatment, so \textcolor{Blue}{$\E[Y|card]-\E[Y|no\;card]$ \textit{is} the ATE} \textit{for this group}.
\smallskip
\item<4->  \textit{Always-takers} are those who will eat pizza regardless of getting the card. 
\textcolor{Blue}{What is $\E[Y|card]-\E[Y|no\;card]$ for this group?}
\smallskip
\item<5-> \textit{Defiers} are those who feel like eating pizza if they don't get a card but when you give them the card, feel like eating salad.  We just assume these people don't exist. 
\end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The Intuition for (Modern) IV}
\onslide<1-> The $ATE_{card}$ is averaged over all units,\\ 
\footnotesize
\begin{align*}
ATE_{card} &= \E_{type}[ATE_{card}|type] \\
&= (ATE_{card}|\text{never-taker})\cdot Pr(\text{never-taker}) \\ 
&+  \textcolor{Blue}{(ATE_{card}|\text{complier})\cdot Pr(complier)} \\
&+ (ATE_{card}|\text{always-taker})\cdot Pr(\text{always-taker}) \\ 
&+ (ATE_{card}|\text{defier})\cdot Pr(\text{defier}) \\
&= 0 + \textcolor{Blue}{(ATE_{card}|\text{complier})\cdot Pr(complier)}  + 0 + 0 \\
\frac{ATE_{card}}{\textcolor{Blue}{Pr(\text{complier})}} 
&= \textcolor{Blue}{(ATE_{card}|\text{complier})} = ATE_{pizza} | \text{complier}
\end{align*}
\small

\bigskip
\onslide<2-> So we just need $Pr(complier)$...
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The Intuition for (Modern) IV}
\small
\onslide<1-> $Pr(complier)$ = prob. take treatment  when encouraged and not otherwise... \\
\bigskip
\onslide<2-> Let 
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item $Z_i$ be the encouragement (card), $D_i$ the treatment (pizza) 
\item $D_{1i}$ and $D_{0i}$ are ``potential treatments'', i.e. whether you take the treatment when encouraged ($D_{1i}$) or not encouraged ($D_{0i}$)
\end{itemize}
\bigskip
\small
\onslide<3-> So $Pr(complier) = Pr(D_{1i}=1)-Pr(D_{0i}=1)= \E[D_{1i}-D_{0i}]$ \\
\bigskip
\onslide<4-> Is this identifiable? \\
\bigskip
\onslide<5-> Yes, by randomization of encouragement!
\begin{align*}
Pr(Complier) &= \E[D_{1i}-D_{0i}] \\
&= \E[D_{i}|Z_i=1]-\E[D_{i}|Z_i=0]
\end{align*}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Saying the Same Thing Many Ways:}
\onslide<1-> We can estimate $ATE$ among compliers,  aka the $LATE$ (local average treatment effect): \\
\footnotesize
\begin{align*}
LATE &= \frac{Reduced Form}{Compliance Ratio}= \frac{ITT}{Pr(complier)} \\
&= \frac{\text{Effect of Z on Y}}{\text{Effect of Z on D}} \\
&= \frac{\E[Y|Z=1]-\E[Y|Z=0]}{\E[D|Z=1]-\E[D|Z=0]} \\
&= \frac{cov(Z,Y)}{var(Z)}/\frac{cov(Z,D)}{var(Z)}\\
&= \frac{(Z^{\top}Z)^{-1}(Z^{\top}Y)}{(Z^{\top}Z)^{-1}(Z^{\top}D)} = (Z^{\top}D)^{-1} (Z^{\top}Y) 
\end{align*}
\smallskip
\normalsize
\begin{center}
\color{Blue}
\onslide<2-> This is the IV, 2SLS, or Wald Estimator
\end{center}
\end{frame}


\begin{frame}{The Intuition for (Modern) IV}
\onslide<1->  Lot's of things can go wrong: \\
\small
\begin{itemize}
\item<2-> We had to assume no effect of $Z$ on $Y$ except through $D$.\\ \textcolor{Blue}{Exclusion restriction.} (\textcolor{Red}{What would go wrong otherwise?}) 
\smallskip
\item<3-> We had to assume $Z$ effects $D$.\\  \textcolor{Blue}{Relevance}. (\textcolor{Red}{What would go wrong otherwise?}) 
\begin{itemize}
\item<3-> Moreover, $Z$ cannot affect $D$ too weakly. (\textcolor{Red}{Why?})
\end{itemize}
\smallskip
\item<4-> We had to assume $Z$ was ``random''\\ \textcolor{Blue}{Ignorability}.  (\textcolor{Red}{What would go wrong otherwise?}) 
\smallskip
\item<5-> We had to assume no defiers\\ \textcolor{Blue}{Monotonicity}. (\textcolor{Red}{What would go wrong otherwise?}) 
\item<6-> If everything goes right, is the effect you estimate a representative treatment effect? 
\end{itemize}
\normalsize
\smallskip
\onslide<7-> \begin{center}
Good habit to think through any IV problem \\ in this ``randomized encouragement'' setup
\end{center}
\end{frame}

\frametitle{Two Views on Instrumental Variables}
\onslide<1-> That's a suitable way to think about instruments. Now let's do it all again with more technicality.  Two ways: 
\bigskip 
\begin{enumerate}
\item<2-> Traditional Econometric Framework
\begin{itemize}
\item<3-> Constant treatment effects
\item<4-> Linearity in the case of a multivalued treatment
\end{itemize}\bigskip \bigskip
\item<5-> Potential Outcome Model of IV
\begin{itemize}
\item<6-> Acknowledges heterogeneous treatment effects
\item<7-> This induced a focus on Local Average Treatment Effect
\item<8-> Most natural for binary treatments
\end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\begin{frame}
  \frametitle{Identification with Traditional Instrumental Variables}
  \onslide<1->
\begin{definition}
Two equations:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textcolor{Blue}{First Stage:} $D=\tau+\rho Z+ \eta$ 
\item \textcolor{Blue}{Second Stage:} $Y=\gamma+\alpha D+ \varepsilon$ 
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}
\onslide<2->
\begin{iass}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Exogeneity and Exclusion: $Cov(Z,\eta)=0$ and $Cov(Z,\varepsilon)=0$
  \item First Stage Relevance: $\rho\neq0$ \footnotesize (typically $F>10$ in first stage regression)
  \normalsize
  \item $\alpha=Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}$ constant for all units $i$.\\ ( \scriptsize or for continuous treatments, $Y_{a',i}-Y_{a,i} = \beta (a'-a)$.)
\end{enumerate}
\end{iass}\vspace{-.2in}
\end{frame}

\section{Instrumental Variables with Potential Outcomes (No Covariates)}

\begin{frame}
  \frametitle{Potential Outcome Model for Instrumental Variables}
\onslide<1-> \begin{definition}[Instrument]
$Z_i$: Binary instrument for {\em unit} $i$.
 \[
 Z_i = \left\{
 \begin{array}{ll}
  1 & \mbox{if unit $i$ ``encouraged'' to receive treatment}\\
  0 & \mbox{if unit $i$ ``encouraged'' to receive control}
 \end{array}
 \right.
 \]
\end{definition}

\onslide<2-> \begin{definition}[Potential Treatments]
 $D_{zi}$ indicates \emph{potential} treatment status given $Z_i=z$
\begin{itemize}
\item e.g. if $D_{1i}=1$ encouraged to take treatment and takes treatment
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\onslide<3-> \begin{assumption}
Observed treatments are realized as
\[
D_i = Z_i \cdot D_{1i}+(1-Z_i)\cdot D_{0i} \,\, \mbox{so}\,\,
D_i = \left\{
 \begin{array}{ll}
  D_{1i} & \mbox{if $Z_i=1$}\\
  D_{0i} & \mbox{if $Z_i=0$}
 \end{array}
 \right.
\]
\end{assumption}
\end{frame}


\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Potential Outcome Model for Instrumental Variables}
Following Angrist, Imbens, and Rubin (1996), we can define:\bigskip
\begin{Definition}
\begin{itemize}
 \item {\em Compliers}: $D_{1i}>D_{0i}$ ($D_{0i}=0$ and $D_{1i}=1$).
 \item {\em Always-takers}: $D_{1i}=D_{0i}=1$.
 \item {\em Never-takers}: $D_{1i}=D_{0i}=0$.
 \item {\em Defiers}: $D_{1i}<D_{0i}$ ($D_{0i}=1$ and $D_{1i}=0$).
\end{itemize}
\end{Definition}
%\bigskip
\begin{Problem}
Only one of the potential treatment indicators
$(D_{0i},D_{1i})$ is observed, so we cannot identify which group any particular individual belongs to
\end{Problem}
\end{frame}

\subsection{Identification}

\begin{frame}
  \frametitle{Potential Outcome Model for Instrumental Variables}
\begin{estm}[LATE]
\small
$$\alpha_{LATE}=\E[Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}|D_{1i}>D_{0i}]$$ is the Local Average Treatment Effect among compliers.\\ \smallskip \footnotesize NB: this estimand varies with the particular instrument $Z$. 
\end{estm}
\normalsize
\onslide<2-> Some special cases: 
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item<2-> When the treatment intake, $D_i$, is itself randomized,\pause then $Z_i=D_i$ and every individual is a complier
\item<3-> If one-sided noncompliance s.t. $D_{0i}=0$: \pause
\end{itemize}
\begin{equation*}
\begin{split}
\E[Y_{1i}|D_{1i}>D_{0i}]&=\E[Y_{1i}|Z_i=1,D_{1i}=1]=\E[Y_{1i}|D_i=1]\\ 
&\mbox{And}\\
\E[Y_{0i}|D_{1i}>D_{0i}]&=\E[Y_{0i}|D_i=1]
\end{split}
\end{equation*}
Thus, 

$$\alpha_{LATE}=\E[Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}|D_{1i}>D_{0i}]=\E[Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}|D_i=1]=\alpha_{ATET}$$
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  \frametitle{Identification with Instrumental Variables}
\begin{iass}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Ignorability of the Instrument: $(Y_{0i},Y_{1i}, D_{0i},D_{1i}) \indep Z_i$
  \item First Stage:  $P(D_{1i}=1) \neq P(D_{0i}=1)$
  \item Monotonicity: $D_{1i} \geq D_{0i}$ for all $i$ (no defiers)
  \end{enumerate}
\end{iass}\vspace{-.2in}
\bigskip
\onslide<2->
\begin{ires}
\footnotesize
\begin{align*}
 \E[Y_1-Y_0|D_1>D_0] &= \frac{\E[Y|Z=1]-\E[Y|Z=0]}{\E[D|Z=1]-\E[D|Z=0]} \\
   &= \frac{\mbox{Intent to Treat Effect of $Z$ on $Y$}}{\mbox{First Stage Effect of $Z$ on $D$}} \\
   &= \frac{\mbox{Intent to Treat Effect}}{\mbox{Proportion of Compliers}}
\end{align*}
\end{ires}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  \frametitle{Identification with Instrumental Variables}
\begin{iass}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Ignorability of the Instrument: $(Y_{0},Y_{1}, D_{0},D_{1}) \indep Z$
  \item First Stage: $0 < P(Z=1) < 1$ and
  $P(D_{1}=1) \neq P(D_{0}=1)$
  \item Monotonicity: $D_{1} \geq D_{0}$
  \end{enumerate}
\end{iass}\vspace{-.2in}
\onslide<2>
\scriptsize
\begin{Proof}
\begin{multline*}
\frac{E[Y|Z=1]-E[Y|Z=0]}{E[D|Z=1]-E[D|Z=0]}= \frac{E[Y_0 + (Y_1-Y_0) D_1|Z=1]-E[Y_0 + (Y_1-Y_0) D_0|Z=0]}{E[D_1|Z=1]-E[D_0|Z=0]}\\
= \frac{E[Y_0 + (Y_1-Y_0) D_1] - E[Y_0 + (Y_1-Y_0) D_0]}{E[D_1]-E[D_0]}=\frac{E[(Y_1-Y_0)(D_1-D_0)]}{E[D_1-D_0]} \\
= \frac{E[Y_1-Y_0|D_1>D_0]P(D_1>D_0)-E[Y_1-Y_0|D_1<D_0]P(D_1<D_0)}{E[D_1-D_0]} \,\mbox{as $(D_1-D_0)$=$(1,0,-1)$}\\
= \frac{E[Y_1-Y_0|D_1>D_0]P[D_1>D_0]}{P(D_1>D_0)}=E[Y_1-Y_0|D_1>D_0]
\end{multline*}
\end{Proof}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
 \frametitle{Comments on Identification Assumptions}
\small
\onslide<1-> Ignorability of the Instrument: $$\{ Y_{0i},Y_{1i}, D_{0i},D_{1i} \} \indep Z_i$$
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item Implies $Z_i$ ``random'', so that the intent to treat effect and first stage effect are causally identified
\item Gets us exclusion too: if $Z_i$ changed outcome through channel other than treatment, it would change $Y_{0i}$ and $Y_{1i}$. (See pset!)
\item Remember, exclusion needed to attribute effect of $Z$ on $Y$ to the effect of $D$ alone. \textcolor{Red}{Never fully testable!}
\item Random assignment \textit{not} sufficient for exclusion. (\textcolor{Blue}{Why not?})
\end{itemize}
\bigskip
\small
\onslide<2-> First Stage/ Relevance \\
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item Implies that the instrument $Z$ induces variation in $D$
\item Fully testable by regressing $D$ on $Z$. Rule of thumb: get $F$-stat over 10 (better: use Anderson-Rubin CIs)
\end{itemize}\bigskip
\small
\onslide<3-> Monotonicity: $D_{1} \geq D_{0}$\\
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item Rules out defiers. Not testable but often qualitatively convincing.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\subsection{Estimation}

\begin{frame}
  \frametitle{Instrumental Variable: Estimators}
\begin{estm}[LATE]\small
\begin{align*}
\E[Y_1-Y_0|D_1>D_0]
&= \frac{\E[Y|Z=1]-\E[Y|Z=0]}{\E[D|Z=1]-\E[D|Z=0]}
\end{align*}
\end{estm}\vspace{-.2in}
\onslide<2>
\bigskip
\begin{esti}[Wald Estimator]\small
The sample analog estimator is:
\[
\left.\left(
\frac{\sum_{i=1}^N Y_iZ_i}{\sum_{i=1}^N Z_i}
-
\frac{\sum_{i=1}^N Y_i(1-Z_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^N (1-Z_i)}
\right)\right/
\left(
\frac{\sum_{i=1}^N D_iZ_i}{\sum_{i=1}^N Z_i}
-
\frac{\sum_{i=1}^N D_i(1-Z_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^N(1-Z_i)}
\right)
\]
\end{esti}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  \frametitle{Instrumental Variable: Estimators}
\onslide<1>
\begin{esti}[Two-Stage Least Squares]\footnotesize
Can also implement Wald Estimator using two-stage least squares regression:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $D=\pi_0 + \pi Z+ \eta$ (First Stage)
\item $Y=\beta_0 +\beta D+\varepsilon$ (Second Stage)
\end{enumerate}
Implies
\begin{align*}
Y &= \beta_0 + \beta (\pi_0 + \pi Z + \eta)  + \varepsilon \\ 
&= (\beta_0 + \beta \pi_0) + \beta \pi Z + (\beta \eta + \varepsilon)
\end{align*}
where $cov(\varepsilon,Z)=0$ due to exclusion and $cov(\eta,Z)=0$ by exogeneity in first stage
\begin{itemize}
\item $\beta$ gives the LATE
\item If you just regressed $Y$ on $Z$, you get effect of $Z$ on $Y$, i.e. $\beta \pi$ (reduced form). Divide this by $\pi$ (compliance ratio or first stage) and you get $\beta$.
\item Don't actually estimate this way, you will get wrong SEs
\end{itemize}

\bigskip
\end{esti}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Instrumental Variable: Estimators}
\onslide<1->
\begin{esti}[IV Estimator]\footnotesize
Standard implementation is the IV estimator:
\[ \hat{\beta}_{IV} = (\Z^{\top} \D)^{-1} \Z^{\top} \Y   \]
\end{esti}
\onslide<2-> 
\begin{enumerate}
\item Typically estimate using \textit{ivreg()} 
\item If ID assumptions only hold after conditioning on $X$, you can add covariates:
\begin{itemize}
\item<3-> they are added to both stages
\smallskip
\item<4-> $\X=[1;X\; \D]$ and $\Z=[1\;\X\;\Z]$  and estimated  by $(\Z^{\top}\X)^{-1}\Z^{\top}\Y$
%\smallskip
%\item<5-> In general, $\beta_{IV}$ estimated in this way does not necessarily have a clear causal interpretation (see Abadie (2003)). 
\end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

\subsection{Adding Covariates}

\begin{frame}
  \frametitle{Instrumental Variables with Covariates}
\begin{itemize}
\item<1-> Sometimes IV identification assumptions may only hold once we condition on a set of pre-treatment characteristics $X$. %\textcolor{Blue}{Example}: earthquakes as instrument may require country FEs \medskip
\medskip
\item<2-> In traditional econometric view of IV this poses no additional problems: treatment effects are assumed to be constant, so  just include $X$ in the 2SLS model\medskip
\item<3-> But with heterogenous treatment effects, IV/2SLS does not formally identify LATE conditional on $X$. 
\medskip
\item<4-> Also hard to interpret, because complier group changes depending on strata of $X$.
\begin{itemize}
\item<5-> One approach: $\kappa$-weighting are weights s.t. any model you run gets you a result as if only estimated for compliers (Abadie, 2003)
\item<6-> When you use these weights to estimate treatment effect, you get ``Local Average Response Function'' (LARF)
\item<7-> But many investigators still ignore this by taking an econometric view, assuming constant treatment effects
\end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Summary of Modern IV}
\begin{itemize}
\small
\item Instruments can give you causal identification where you don't have SOO or other options. 
\smallskip
\item The key concern is usually the exclusion restriction:
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item together with first-stage ignorability, captured by $$ \{ Y_{0i},Y_{1i}, D_{0i},D_{1i} \} \indep Z_i $$
\item a randomized $Z_i$ is a good start, but not sufficient
\item plausibility tests possible, but cannot be confirmed on observables
\end{itemize}
\small
\item Generally useful to think about in terms of an ``encouragement'' to take the treatment and rescaling the ITT (RF) by the compliance ratio
\smallskip
\item If you do not assume constant treatment effects, then you must recognize the estimate as a LATE among the compliers
%\smallskip
%\item Covariates complicate things: may want to either consider LARF/$\kappa$-weighting or rely on constant effect assumption
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}
