\begin{frame}{Flavors of causality}
  
\onslide<1-> \textbf{Effect of causes.} One kind of causal question the most common type of causal question in applied areas-regards the \textit{effect} of some potential cause, e.g. what is the effect of:\\
\medskip
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
 \item political institutions on corruption?
 \item effect of a drug or other therapy on a health outcome
 \item an ad on purchasing behavior
 \item peace-keeping missions on peace?
 \item body cameras on police behavior?
\end{itemize}
\normalsize
\onslide<2-> \textbf{Causes of effects/ causal attribution}. Another set of questions asks ``what led to an observed outcome''.\\
\bigskip
\onslide<3-> Other/more general/complicated concepts can be embedded in a deep enough framework (namely structural causal models)
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{The Potential Outcomes Approach in a Nutshell}
\small
\onslide<1-> How would you know something had a causal effect? What does that mean?\\
\medskip
\onslide<2-> The POM answer:
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item<2-> Suppose unit $i$ takes a treatment and you measure the outcome. Let's call this \textcolor{Blue}{$Y_{1i}$}, or \textcolor{Blue}{the treatment potential outcome}
\item<3-> You'd like to know how $Y_{1i}$ compares to the value of $Y$ unit $i$ \textit{would have had, if it had not taken the treatment}. Call this \textcolor{Green}{$Y_{0i}$}, or \textcolor{Green}{the non-treatment potential outcome} 
\item<4-> Then unit $i$'s \textit{treatment effect} is $\tau_i = \textcolor{Blue}{Y_{1i}}-\textcolor{Green}{Y_{0i}}$.
\item<5-> The trouble is you do not get to see both \textcolor{Blue}{$Y_{1i}$} and \textcolor{Green}{$Y_{0i}$} for unit $i$. This is the \textcolor{Red}{fundamental problem of causal inference}.
\item<6-> Everything we do will be about filling in the ``missing'' potential outcome for each unit through various \textbf{assumptions}. That is it! 
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Fundamental Quantities}
\small
\begin{itemize}
\item $Y_{1i}$ and $Y_{0i}$, the potential outcomes
\item $D_i$, the treatment status of unit $i$
\item  Observed outcome, $Y_i=D_i Y_{1i}+(1-D_i)Y_{0i}$
\item $\tau_i= Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}$
\end{itemize}
\onslide<2->  For each, discuss whether they are \textcolor{blue}{always observable} or \textcolor{blue}{sometimes observable} or \textcolor{blue}{never observable}.
\begin{enumerate}
\item<3-> $Y_{1i}$ and $Y_{0i}$ (at same time)
\item<4-> $D_i$
\item<5-> $Y_i$
\item<6-> $\tau_i$
\end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Quantities of interest}
Using these, we will soon be interested in things like:\\
\begin{itemize}
\makebiggaps
\item Average treatment effect (ATE): $\E[Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}]=\E[\tau]$
\item Average treatment effect among treated (ATET)
\[\E[\tau_i|D_i=1]=\E[Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}|D_i=1]\]
\item Average treatment effect among controls (ATEC)
\[\E[\tau_i|D_i=0]=\E[Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}|D_i=0]\]
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{\textcolor{Red}{Warning}: regression (or comparison-based) goggles}
\small
\begin{itemize}
\item You are used to thinking $D$ may be associated with $Y$, especially so if $D$ affects $Y$.

\item The \textit{potential} outcomes are not the outcomes: they don't change when you change treatment, they say what would happen under a given treatment.

\item In other words: the treatment doesn't change the potential outcomes, it changes which potential outcomes you see. 

\item The potential outcomes can be independent of $D_i$
\end{itemize}
\onslide<2-> If I haven't made it clear:  we don't really care about group comparisons, we care about within-unit counterfactual comparisons.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Embodied assumptions}
\onslide<1-> Just by writing down POs using notation $Y_i(d)$ we assert:\\
\small
\begin{itemize}
\item<2-> consistency: $Y_i(d)$ is the $Y_i$ you'd see if $i$ received treatment $d$.
\medskip
\item<3-> no-interference: we've only asked if unit $i$ took the treatment, not unit $j$, so presumably $Y_i(D_i,D_j)= Y_i(D_i)$ regardless of the value of $D_j$. 
\medskip
\item<4-> one-version-of-treatment -- $d$ means the same thing all the time...but we can almost always define our way around this so it is not much of a problem.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Embodied assumptions}
\onslide<1-> Consistency is definitional to POs...but we name the idea because:\\
\small
\begin{itemize}
\item<2-> we will need to reference it later (as in $\E[Y_{1i}|D_i=1]=\E[Y_i|D_i=1]$)
\medskip
\item<3-> to remind ourselves it can breakdown, e.g. due to different treatment versions or interference.
\end{itemize}
\bigskip
\normalsize
\onslide<4-> Non-interference + one-version are usually stated as part of Stable Unit Treatment Value assumption (SUTVA, Rubin 1980) \\
\bigskip
\onslide<5-> We can relax these in various ways, including:
\small
\begin{itemize}
\item ``treatment variation irrelevance'' (VanderWeele 2009) or redefining treatment
\item expanding POs to index treatment of others in some limited way
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Problematic POs}
\onslide<1-> Just because you can write a PO doesn't mean you should.\\
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item<2-> with a PO we imagine changing the treatment status of a unit
\item<3-> one complication is that when you change the treatment status of unit $i$ it can affect a lot of things and this can confuse the causal quantity we are after
\item<4-> heuristic: if you cannot imagine how you would intervene to change the treatment status, it may signal ill-definedness.
\end{itemize} 
\bigskip
\small
\onslide<5-> Example: what is the effect of leader gender on some outcome? \\
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item<5-> for US presidents, this involves $Y_{trump}(female)$, i.e. ``What would Donald Trump have done if he was female?'
\item<7-> ``Changing Trump to female'' would change countless subsequent experiences (and the probability of being in the sample of presidents anyway).
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Problematic POs}
\onslide<1-> In other cases, a concept may seem clear and modifiable yet we may be unclear about what exactly it would change (e.g. obesity)\\
\bigskip
\onslide<2-> Later:  you will see that our intuitions about potential outcomes and how they relate to observed variables can go wrong once you start conditioning on things (including sample selection)...graphical approaches will help us see that.\\
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Repeat and refine: Quantities of usual interest}
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item<2-> Treatment effect, $\tau_i = Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}$
\item<3-> Average Treatment Effect (ATE):  
\[ATE = \E[Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}] = \E[\tau_i] = \int \tau p(\tau) d\tau \]
\item<4-> Average treatment effect on the treated (ATT):
\[ATT = \E[Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}|D_i=1] = \int \tau p(\tau|D=1) d\tau\]
\item<5-> Average treatment effect on the controls (ATC):
\[ATC = \E[Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}|D_i=0] = \int \tau p(\tau|D=0) d\tau\]
\item<6-> Average treatment effect for sub-groups ($ATE(X)$):
\[ATE(X) = \E[Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}|X=x] = \int \tau p(\tau|X=x) d\tau \]
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
\frametitle{Back to the SUTVA}
\small
\onslide<1-> Again, by writing POs as $\{Y_{1i},Y_{0i}\}$ we imply we only care about treatment status of $i$, not $j$. \\
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item And we defined the causal effect $\tau_i$ as $Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}$
\end{itemize}
\small
\medskip
\onslide<2-> Words you'll hear: Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA), ``no interference'', or ``individualized treatment response''. \\ 
\medskip
\onslide<3-> It is easy to break: disease, vaccination, conflict, education, anti-corruption monitoring, ... \\
\medskip
\onslide<4-> Without this you get a proliferation of potential outcomes. E.g. with four units you could define:\\
\[\{Y_i(0,0,0,0), Y_i(0,0,0,1),..., Y_i(1,1,1,1)\}\]
\vspace{-.2in}
\begin{itemize}
\item<5-> how many potential outcomes for each unit, $i$? \\
\item<6-> and how many causal effects for $i$?
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Potential outcomes with interference}
\small
\begin{definition}[SUTVA]
Unit $i$'s potential outcomes depend on $D_i$ and not on $D_{j \neq i}$
\end{definition}
\begin{itemize}
\item<2-> e.g. $Y_3(0,0,D_3,0)$ is same as $Y_3(0,1,D_3,1)$, $Y_3(1,1,D_3,0)$,... 
\item<3-> So just write $Y_3(D_3)$
\item<4-> Also limits the causal effects for each unit: simply $\tau_i=Y_i(1)-Y_i(0)=Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}$
\end{itemize}
\bigskip
\onslide<5-> This is an example of an \textbf{exclusion restriction}: we use outside information to rule out possibility of certain
causal effects.
\begin{itemize}
\item<6-> the effect of you taking the treatment on my POs is excluded (0)
\item<6-> traditional models (e.g. regression) do same: $Y_i$ depends on $X_i$ not $X_j$.
\end{itemize}
\onslide<7-> Causal inference in the presence of interference is an
area of active research. e.g. sometimes ``spillover'' or ``saturation'' is deliberately varied 
\end{frame}

\end{itemize}

\begin{frame}{Selection bias}
\footnotesize
\begin{align*}
\E[Y_i|D_i=1]-\E[Y_i|D_i=0] &= \E[Y_{1i}|D_i=1]-\E[Y_{0i}|D_i=0] \\
&= \textcolor{Blue}{\E[Y_{1i}-Y_{0i}|D_i=1]} + \textcolor{Green}{\left(\E[Y_{0i}|D_i=1]-\E[Y_{0i}|D_i=0] \right)} \\
&= \textcolor{Blue}{\text{ATT}} + \textcolor{Green}{\text{selection bias}}
\end{align*}  \\
\smallskip
\onslide<2-> Example: $D=\text{Church Attendance}$, $Y=\text{Political Participation}$ \\
\scriptsize
\begin{itemize}
\item Church goers may differ from non-Church goers on a range of background characteristics (e.g.~civic duty)
\smallskip
\item Should you believe the naive DIM, $\E[Y_i|D_i=1]-\E[Y_i|D_i=0]$, reflects the causal effect of the church experience exclusively? 
\smallskip
\item Why not?...there could be some reason these groups are different ``already'', i.e. absent any effect of church itself: \textcolor{Green}{$\text{bias}=\E[Y_{0i}|D_i=1]-\E[Y_{0i}|D_i=0] \neq 0$}
\end{itemize}
\smallskip
\footnotesize
\onslide<3-> Example: Human rights treaties\\
\scriptsize
\begin{itemize}
\item Maybe countries willing to sign a human rights treaty have better human rights (absent any effect of the treaty itself): \textcolor{Green}{$\text{bias}=\E[Y_{0i}|D_i=1]-\E[Y_{0i}|D_i=0] \neq 0$}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Assignment mechanism}
\small
\onslide<1-> We often start an analysis by thinking about how units are assigned to treatment. \\
\medskip
\onslide<2-> We can classify many important examples (similar to the graphical examples we tried) by what we know of the assignment mechanism (for $D_i$):
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item When we know it was random, we know those with $D=1$ and $D=0$ are ``comparable'', meaning same average POs. 
\smallskip
\item We previewed that if $D$ is random conditionally on observed $X$, we can manage.
\smallskip
\item When we don't know anything about assignment, \textcolor{Blue}{the data say nothing about the causal effects without importing some other assumption}.
\end{itemize}
\small
\medskip
\onslide<4-> Those represent important assignment mechanisms with hallowed names in varied fields: 
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item random assignment, ignorability, or exchangeability 
\item selection on observables, conditional ignorability
\item bad news
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}


\begin{frame}{Summing Up: \\The Neyman-Rubin / Potential Outcome Model}
\small
\begin{itemize}
\item<1-> Defines causality through counterfactual comparisons, defines various possible causal quantities of interest.
\medskip
\item<2-> In so doing, forces us to think about potential outcomes rather than just the observed outcome.
\medskip
\item<3-> There is no progress without estimating missing potential outcomes.
\scriptsize
\begin{itemize}
\item<4-> how do the potential outcomes we see differ from those we don't?
\item<5-> answer often comes through knowledge of the assignment mechanism, $p(D|Y_1, Y_0, X)$
\end{itemize}
\small
\medskip
\item<6-> Writing POs commits us to embodied assumptions such as consistency and non-interference.
\end{itemize}
\onslide<8-> \textcolor{Blue}{From here}: random assignment, then random-conditional-on-observables.
\end{frame}
