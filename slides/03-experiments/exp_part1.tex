\begin{frame}
  \frametitle{Randomization Solves the Selection Problem}
  \small
Recall this formula for the bias of the DIM ($\tilde{\tau}$) relative to ATT: 
\begin{eqnarray*}
\tilde{\tau} & = & \E[Y_i|D_i=1]-\E[Y_i|D_i=0] \quad \text{(obs. diff. in means)} \\
 & = & \E[Y_{1i} | D_i=1]-\E[Y_{0i} | D_i=0] \\
 & = & \underbrace{\E[Y_{1i} - Y_{0i} | D_i=1]}_{\tau_{ATT}}
                 +\underbrace{\E[Y_{0i} | D_i=1]-\E[Y_{0i} | D_i=0]}_{\mbox{Bias}}
\end{eqnarray*}
\pause
Stare at the bias term and ask: What could ensure it is zero?
\pause
\medskip

\alert{Random assignment} of $D_i$ will make the treated and untreated units identical on average, such that
$$ \E[Y_{0i}\mid D_i=1] \ = \ \E[Y_{0i}\mid D_i=0] $$

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Prelude}
\small
From ``Experimentation and social interventions: a forgotten but important history'' (Ann Oakley, 1998, \textit{BMJ}). From the summary points:

\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}

\item<1-> Many social scientists argue that randomised controlled trials are inappropriate for evaluating social interventions, but they ignore a considerable history, mainly in the United States, of the use of randomised controlled trials to assess different approaches to public policy and health promotion

\item<2-> A tradition of experimental sociology was well established by the 1930s, built on the early use of controlled experiments in psychology and education

\item<3-> From the early 1960s to early 1980s randomised experiments were considered the optimal design for evaluating public policy interventions in the United States, and major evaluations using this design were carried out.\footnote{Me: Also note Cambpbell \& Stanley published ``Experimental and Quasi-experimental designs for research'' in 1966)}

\item<4-> This approach became less popular as policy makers reacted negatively to evidence of ``near zero'' effects

\item<5-> Lessons to be learnt about implementing randomised controlled trials in real life settings include the difficulty of assessing complex multi-level interventions and the challenge of integrating qualitative data

\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  \frametitle{Recall from last time:  Identification vs. Estimation}
  \small
Goal of causal inference: Learn about an \textit{unobservable, counterfactual-dependent} quantity of interest (QoI) using {\it observed} data.\\

\pause

\medskip
Two inferential hurdles:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \alert{Identification}: If you can observe data from an entire {\it population}, can you learn about your QoI?
\item \alert{Estimation}: Given your finite amount of data on a {\it sample}, how well can you learn about your QoI?
\end{enumerate}
\pause
\begin{center}
Golden rule of inference: \textbf{Identification precedes estimation}
\end{center}

\begin{frame}
\frametitle{Classical Randomized Experiment}
\small
Setup:
\begin{itemize}
\item Units: $i = 1, ..., N$
\item Treatment: $D_i \in \{0,1\}$, randomly assigned
\item Potential outcomes: $Y_{0i}$, $Y_{1i}$
\item Observed outcome: $Y_i = Y_{D_i{i}}$
\item Number of treated/untreated units: $N_1 = \sum_{i=1}^N D_i$ and $N_0 = N - N_1$
\end{itemize}

\pause
Notes:
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item Random assignment can take one of several forms:
	\begin{itemize}
	\scriptsize
	\makegaps
	\item \alert{Complete randomization}: Exactly $N_1$ treated units
	\item \alert{Simple (Bernoulli) randomization}: Each unit independently assigned to treatment with probability $p$	
	\end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\begin{frame}[t]
\frametitle{Identification of Average Treatment Effect Under Experiments}
\small
\onslide<1-> \textcolor{Blue}{Identification Assumption}: (guaranteed by random assignment):
$$ \l\{ Y_{1i}, \ Y_{0i}\r\} \ \indep \ D_i $$ \\

\onslide<2-> \textcolor{Blue}{Quantity of Interest}: \\
\vspace{-.2in}
$$ \tau_{ATE} \ \equiv \ \E[Y_{1i} - Y_{0i}]$$
\onslide<3-> \textcolor{Blue}{How does our ID assumption lead to identification of $\tau_{ATE}$}?\\ 
\footnotesize
\onslide<4-> \begin{eqnarray*}
\E[Y_{1i}] - \E[Y_{0i}] &=& \E[Y_{1i}|D_i=1] - \E[Y_{0i} | D_i=0] \quad (\text{why?}) \\
&=& \E[Y_{i}|D_i=1] - \E[Y_{i} | D_i=0]  \quad (\text{why?})\\
&=& \text{DIM Estimand}\\
&\approx & \overline{Y}_1 - \overline{Y}_0   \quad (\text{the DIM estimator})
\end{eqnarray*}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{SATE and PATE}
\begin{itemize}
\itemsep1pt\parskip0pt\parsep0pt
\item<1->
 Often we focus on estimating the average causal effect in a
  particular sample: \textbf{S}ample \textbf{A}verage \textbf{T}reatment
  \textbf{E}ffect (SATE)\medskip

  \begin{itemize}
   \item uncertainty arises only from hypothetical randomizations.\medskip
  \item  inferences are limited to the sample in our study. 
  \end{itemize}\bigskip
\item<2-> Compare to the \textbf{P}opulation
  \textbf{A}verage \textbf{T}reatment \textbf{E}ffect (PATE)\medskip
 \begin{itemize}
  \item requires knowledge about the sampling process that got you from population to sample\medskip
\item need to account for two sources of variation:\medskip
  \begin{itemize}
  \item variation from the sampling process\medskip
  \item variation from treatment assignment (randomness of $D_i$) 
  \end{itemize}
  \end{itemize}\bigskip
  
 
\item<3->  \small  If your sampling was random from a given population, then $\E[SATE]=PATE$. For this reason, we're often uninterested in distinguishing the two quantities.

\end{itemize}

\begin{frame}{Uncertainty (SE) Estimates for SATE and PATE}
\onslide<1-> A natural first attack: pretending
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item Imagine you have a large enough sample that you can draw partition it and draw the treated from one and the control from the other
\item Compute $\overline{Y}_1$ and $\overline{Y}_0$ based on these separate samples.
\item Chance variation in the two then unrelated: $cov(\overline{Y}_1,\overline{Y}_0)= 0$ 
\end{itemize}
\bigskip
\onslide<2-> Get variance of the difference in means:
\begin{align*}
\V({\overline{Y}_1 -\overline{Y}_0}) &= \V(\overline{Y}_1)+\V(\overline{Y}_0)-2cov(\overline{Y}_1,\overline{Y}_0)\\
 &= \frac{\sigma^2_{Y1}}{N_1} + \frac{\sigma^2_{Y0}}{N_0} \\
SE_{ATE} &= \sqrt{\frac{\sigma^2_{Y1}}{N_1} + \frac{\sigma^2_{Y0}}{N_0}}
\end{align*}
\footnotesize
where $\sigma^2_{Y1}$ and $\sigma^2_{Y0}$ are the variance of the $Y_{1i}$ and $Y_{0i}$ in the population
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
\small 
We had, 
\footnotesize
\begin{align*}
SE_{ATE} &= \sqrt{\frac{\sigma^2_{Y1}}{N_1} + \frac{\sigma^2_{Y0}}{N_0}}
\end{align*}
\small
\onslide<2-> A suitable analog estimator being,
	\footnotesize
\begin{align*}
\widehat{SE}_{ATE} &= \sqrt{\frac{\hat{\sigma}^2_{Y1}}{N_1} + \frac{\hat{\sigma}^2_{Y0}}{N_0}}
\end{align*}
\smallskip \\
\small
\onslide<3-> This is what we typically use, known as the Neyman estimator and it is
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item the correct SE for the PATE 
\item what you get from t-tests (unequal variance), or regression with robust SE
\item conservative for the SATE (where the sample is fixed but $D_i$ is random)
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}


\begin{frame}
\frametitle{The painful truth}
\small
\onslide<1-> In reality we can only assign treatment within a finite sample so expect $cov(\overline{Y}_1,\overline{Y}_0) \neq 0$.\\

\medskip
\onslide<2-> Considering this, under complete randomization the true standard error of the sample ATE is actually
\pause
\footnotesize
 \begin{eqnarray*}
 SE_{\widehat{ATE}}&=&\sqrt{ \left( \frac{N-N_1}{N-1}\right) \frac{\sigma^2_{Y1}}{N_1}+ \left( \frac{N-N_0}{N-1}\right) \frac{\sigma^2_{Y0}}{N_0} + \left(\frac{1}{N-1} \right) 2Cov[Y_{1},Y_{0}]} \\
% SE_{\widehat{ATE}}&=&\sqrt{ \frac{1}{N-1} \left(\frac{N_1}{N_0}Var[Y_1]+\frac{N_0}{N_1}Var[Y_1]+2Cov[Y_1,Y_0]\right)} 
\end{eqnarray*}
\vspace{-.1in}
\small
with population variances and covariances $\sigma^2_{Yd}$, $Cov(Y_{1},Y_{0})$.\\

%Var[Y_{di}]\equiv\frac{1}{N}\sum_1^N\left(Y_{di}-\frac{\sum_1^N Y_{di}}{N}\right)^2=\sigma^2_{Y_{d}|D_i=d}

%Cov[Y_{1i},Y_{0i}]\equiv\frac{1}{N} \sum_1^N \left(Y_{1i}-\frac{\sum_1^N Y_{1i}}{N}\right) \left(Y_{0i}-\frac{\sum_1^N Y_{0i}}{N}\right)=\sigma^2_{Y_1,Y_0}

\begin{itemize}
\item<1-> Good news: the simple/Neyman estimator we derived above is conservative for the SATE 
\bigskip
\item<2-> It is just right for the PATE, which should make sense given how we arrived at it.
\end{itemize}

\begin{frame}{Proof}
\small
Two preliminaries:
\footnotesize
\begin{itemize}
\item $Cov(Y_1,Y_0) \leq SD(Y_1)SD(Y_0)$
\item $Var(Y_1) + Var(Y_0) \geq 2\sqrt{Var(Y_1) Var(Y_0)}$
\end{itemize}
\small
\onslide<2-> With that,
\scriptsize
\begin{eqnarray*}
  SE_{\widehat{ATE}}  & = & \sqrt{ \left( \frac{N-N_1}{N-1}\right) \frac{Var[Y_{1}]}{N_1}+ \left( \frac{N-N_0}{N-1}\right) \frac{Var[Y_{0}]}{N_0} + \left(\frac{1}{N-1} \right) 2Cov[Y_{1},Y_{0}]} \\
 & = & \sqrt{ \frac{1}{N-1} \left(\frac{N_0}{N_1}Var[Y_1]+\frac{N_1}{N_0}Var[Y_0]+2Cov[Y_1,Y_0]\right)}  \\
                      & \leq & \sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1}\left( \frac{N_0}{N_1}Var[Y_1]+\frac{N_1}{N_0}Var[Y_0] +2\sqrt{Var[Y_1]Var[Y_0]}\right)} \\
                     & \leq & \sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1}\left( \frac{N_0}{N_1}Var[Y_1]+\frac{N_1}{N_0}Var[Y_0]  + Var[Y_1]+Var[Y_0]\right)} \\
\end{eqnarray*}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  \frametitle{$SE_{\widehat{ATE}}\leq \widehat{SE}_{\widehat{ATE}}$, continued.}
\scriptsize
\begin{eqnarray*}
\scriptsize
% \nonumber to remove numbering (before each equation)
  SE_{\widehat{ATE}} & \leq &\sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1}\left( \frac{N_0}{N_1}Var[Y_1]+\frac{N_1}{N_0}Var[Y_0]  + Var[Y_1]+Var[Y_0]\right)} \\
  & \leq & \sqrt{ \frac{N_0^2Var[Y_1]+N_1^2 Var[Y_0] + N_1 N_0 (Var[Y_1]+Var[Y_0])}{(N-1)N_1 N_0}} \\
  & \leq & \sqrt{ \frac{(N_0^2 + N_1 N_0) Var[Y_1]+(N_1^2 + N_1 N_0) Var[Y_0] }{(N-1)N_1 N_0}} \\
%  & \leq & \sqrt{ \frac{(N-m)^2Var[Y_1]+m^2Var[Y_0] + m(N-m)(Var[Y_1]+Var[Y_0])}{(N-1)m(N-m)}} \\
    & \leq & \sqrt{ \frac{(N_0 + N_1) N_0 Var[Y_1]}{(N-1) N_1 N_0}+ \frac{ (N_1 + N_0) N_1  Var[Y_0]}{(N-1)N_1 N_0}}  \\
    & \leq & \sqrt{ \frac{N \,  Var[Y_1]}{(N-1) N_1 }+ \frac{ N \, Var[Y_0]}{(N-1) N_0}}  \\
%    & = & \sqrt{ \frac{N(N-m) Var[Y_1]}{(N-1)m(N-m)}+ \frac{m N Var[Y_0]}{(N-1)m(N-m)}}  \\
    & \leq & \sqrt{\frac{N}{N-1} \left(\frac{Var[Y_1]}{N_1}+ \frac{ Var[Y_0]}{(N_0)}\right)}  \\
    &\leq & \sqrt{ \left(\frac{Var[Y_1]}{N_1}+ \frac{ Var[Y_0]}{(N_0)}\right)}  
%    & \leq & \sqrt{ \frac{ \widehat{\sigma}^2_{Y|D_i=1}  }{N_1}+ \frac{ \widehat{\sigma}^2_{Y|D_i=0} }{(N_0)}}
\end{eqnarray*}
\small
$\therefore$ our estimator is conservative.

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}

\begin{itemize}
\item<4-> you likely know already:
\item<5-> 1,159 is more than 2 SEs from 0, so you know $t$-stat/ $z$-score would be over 2 and p-value would be less than 0.05.

\end{itemize}

\begin{frame}[fragile]
\frametitle{Testing the Null Hypothesis of Zero Average Effect}
\small
\onslide<1-> Use a two-sample t (or z) test with unequal variances:\[
t=\frac{\widehat{\tau}}{\sqrt{\displaystyle\frac{\widehat{\sigma}_{Y_i|D_i=1}^2}{N_1} + \displaystyle\frac{\widehat{\sigma}_{Y_i|D_i=0}^2}{N_0}}}=\frac{\$1,159}{\sqrt{\displaystyle\frac{\$17,038^2}{7,487} + \displaystyle\frac{\$16,180^2}{3,717}}}\approx3.5
\]
\begin{itemize}
\footnotesize
\item For large enough $N$, $t \stackrel{d}{\rightarrow} N(0,1)$, 
\item Getting a 3.5 or above from a normal distribution would be quite a shock:

\end{verbatim}
\footnotesize
\item Inverting the test statistic we can construct a 95\% confidence interval
\[

\]
\item What assumptions did we need along the way?
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}

\begin{esti}[Regression with Experiments]\small
The ATE can be expressed as a regression coefficient:
\begin{eqnarray*}

   &=& Y_{0i} + (Y_{1i}- Y_{0i})\,D_i \\

\end{eqnarray*}

  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\begin{frame}\frametitle{Testing in Small Samples: Fisher's Exact Test}
\begin{itemize}
\item Test of differences in means with large $N$:
\small
\[
H_0:\E[Y_1] =\E[Y_0],\quad H_1:\E[Y_1] \neq\E[Y_0]  \mbox{  (weak null)}
\]\pause
\normalsize
\item Fisher's Exact Test:
\small
\[
H_0: Y_{1i} = Y_{0i},\pause\quad H_1: Y_1 \neq Y_0\;\; \forall i,\;\;\qquad\mbox{(sharp null of no effect)}
\]\pause
\end{itemize}
\normalsize
\vspace{-.2in}
\onslide<4-> \alert{Key idea}: Under the sharp null, we ``observe'' all potential outcomes -- can compute what ATE would be under alternate randomizations \\
\pause
\medskip
...which allows you to see many effect estimates all under this null.
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}\frametitle{Testing in Small Samples: Fisher's Exact Test}
\small
\onslide<1-> Let $\Omega$ be the set of all possible ways to assign treatments. \\
\bigskip
\onslide<2-> Fisher's exact test procedure:\\
\footnotesize
\begin{enumerate}
\item<3->  Calculate a statistic $\hat{\theta}_{true}$ (e.g. difference in means) from original treatment assignment data
\item<4->  Obtain the null distribution of the statistic by calculating the same statistic $\hat{\theta}(\omega)$ under the sharp null for every possible (or many) $\omega$ in $\Omega$ 
\item<5-> Compare $\hat{\theta}_{true}$ to the null distribution of $\hat{\theta}(\omega)$'s to see how ``extreme'' it is
\end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

\end{overprint}

\section{Threats to Validity}


\begin{frame}
  \frametitle{Threats to Internal and External Validity for Experiments}
\begin{itemize}
\item<1-> \alert{Internal validity}: can we estimate the treatment effect for our particular sample?
\begin{itemize}
\footnotesize
\item Fails when there are differences between treated and controls (other than the treatment itself) that affect the outcome
\item In other words, how might the randomization have failed? (See next)
\end{itemize}\bigskip
\normalsize
\item<2-> \alert{External validity}: can we extrapolate our	estimates to other populations?
\begin{itemize}
\footnotesize
\item Might $Y_{1i}$, $Y_{0i}$, and $\tau_i$ have looked different in a different part of the population?
\end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  \frametitle{Most Common Threats to Internal Validity}
\begin{itemize}
\item<1-> \textcolor{Blue}{Randomization fails at assignment}
\footnotesize
 \begin{itemize}
   \item e.g. implementing partners assign their favorites to treatment group; imbalance due to small sample size
%   \item In JTPA: Reasonably good balance
 \end{itemize}\bigskip
\normalsize
 \item<2-> \textcolor{Blue}{Noncompliance with assignment}
\footnotesize
 \begin{itemize}
   \item e.g. failure to treat or ``crossover''. Some members of the control group receive the treatment and some members of the treatment group go untreated
\item e.g. in JTPA: only 62\% of women and 66\% of men assigned for treatment actually enrolled in JTPA training (on the other side, compliance was almost perfect in the control group).
 \end{itemize}\bigskip
\normalsize
 \item<3-> \textcolor{Blue}{Differential attrition}
\footnotesize
 \begin{itemize}
 \item attrition \textit{can} be okay, even if different rates for treated and contols
 \item but, if probability of attrition depends on treatment status \textbf{and} potential outcomes, you break the randomization
 \item Two scenarios: (i) people attrite from treatment-taking, but you still get to measure outcome; (ii) people attrite and you can't measure any outcome for them post-treatment.
\end{itemize}\bigskip
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  \frametitle{Most Common Threats to External Validity}
\begin{itemize}
 \item<1-> \textcolor{Blue}{Non-representative sample}
 \medskip
  \begin{itemize}
  \footnotesize
  \addtolength{\itemsep}{.5\baselineskip}
   \item e.g. laboratory experiment using a convenience sample
   \item units are randomly assigned, but not from the population of interest
 \end{itemize}\bigskip
\normalsize
 \item<2-> \textcolor{Blue}{Non-representative treatment}
 \medskip
  \begin{itemize}
  \footnotesize
  \addtolength{\itemsep}{.5\baselineskip}
   \item the treatment differs from what you would actual implement
   \item<3-> e.g. survey experiment about the effect of media priming on voting
   \item<4-> scale effects
   \item<5-> actual treatments may be bundled
  \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
  \frametitle{Internal vs. External Validity}
\footnotesize
Which one is more important?\\\bigskip \pause

``One common view is that internal validity comes first. If you do not know the
effects of the treatment on the units in your study, you are not well-positioned to
infer the effects on units you did not study who live in circumstances you did not study.'' (Rosenbaum 2010, p. 56)\\\bigskip \pause

\begin{itemize}
\item Randomization (sometimes) ensures internal validity
\item External validity may be partially addressed by comparing the results of several
internally valid studies conducted in different circumstances and at different times
\item While experiments can be especially narrow, same external validity issues often apply in observational work
\end{itemize}
\onslide<3-> That said,\\
\begin{itemize}
\item<4-> Often a studies with lower internal validity give us the theory, inspiration, qualitative knowledge needed for an experiment.
\item<5-> Our aim is to get away from ``valid'' and ``not valid'' to instead discuss the evidence that a given conclusion would hold.
\end{itemize}
\end{frame}
